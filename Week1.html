Week 1: Math review

Some very basic real analysis

As you know, functions can be represented by either an infinite or finite sum of other functions. In the former case, this is true if the infinite series evaluates to something finite - it is convergent rather than divergent. In class I only went through the Taylor expansion where the convergence property is rather obvious. Let’s now review the more complete picture.

1.1 I’m assuming you all know and remember how to deal with a series of numbers. We extend that notion such that each element of the sum is a function, uiu_iui​, of variable xxx. The notion of a partial sum is important here:
σn(x):=∑inui(x) \sigma_n(x):=\sum^n_iu_i(x) σn​(x):=i∑n​ui​(x)
Then the infinite sum is defined as the limit of infinite nnn, σn→∞(x)=f(x)\sigma_{n\to \infty}(x)=f(x)σn→∞​(x)=f(x). If this limit exists, the sum is convergent. But how does the existence of this limit depend on the variable xxx? Well, if it doesn’t then the series is said to be uniformly convergent.

Let’s say this exact same thing formally:
If ∀ϵ>0\forall \epsilon>0∀ϵ>0 and x∈[a,b]x\in[a,b]x∈[a,b], ∃N∈Z+\exists N\in \mathbb{Z}_+∃N∈Z+​ such that ∣f(x)−σn>N(x)∣<ϵ|f(x)-\sigma_{n>N}(x)|<\epsilon∣f(x)−σn>N​(x)∣<ϵ, the series converges to f(x)f(x)f(x) uniformly on interval [a,b][a,b][a,b].

The concept of non-uniform convergence comes up usually near the boundaries of the interval of convergence where there must be some constraint on xxx that renders the sum finite.

I’ll remind you that series can also either be absolutely or conditionally convergent. A series, ∑i∞ui(x)\sum_i^\infty u_i(x)∑i∞​ui​(x) is absolutely convergent if ∑i∞∣ui(x)∣<∞\sum_i^\infty |u_i(x)|<\infty∑i∞​∣ui​(x)∣<∞. If a series is convergent but not absolutely convergent, it is conditionally convergent.
Absolute/conditional convergence is a distinct notion from uniform convergence!

As you probably recall, there are tests of all kinds of convergence. Two popular tests for uniform convergence is the Weierstrass M test (very simple to prove and implement) and Abel’s test (rather subtle but important for power series).

Uniform convergence is very important to check because it implies f(x)f(x)f(x) and ui(x)u_i(x)ui​(x) are at least continuous and the series can be integrated or differentiated term-wise.

1.2 The first note is to remind you about the notion of convergence which you should always have in the back of your mind. Practically, what’s more relevant is constructing these function series in a useful way. In physics useful will typically mean

  • Approximate integrals by replacing the integrand with a series expansion
  • Solve ODEs by choosing an appropriate power series expansion of the solution
  • Approximate ODEs by perturbation series

Differential vector calculus

2.1 Consider a set together with two operations - addition and scalar multiplication. Roughly, such a set is a vector space with each element called a vector if the sum of of two vectors is again a vector and multiplication by a scalar rescales the vector. There is an additive inverse (−v⃗=v⃗−1-\vec v=\vec v^{-1}−v=v−1), additive identity (0⃗\vec 00) and scalar identity (111). There are two more operators we may consider:

  • The scalar product which assigns a number to two vectors (technically one vector and one co-vector).
  • The cross product takes two vectors into a vector normal to the plane spanned by the two vectors where normality is defined relative to the inner product.

Let’s focus on vector in Euclidean space, Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd where a vector is, morally speaking, a direction and a magnitude. In this simple review we choose the intuitive basis
v⃗=∑i=1dvie^i \vec v =\sum_{i=1}^dv_i\hat e_i v=i=1∑d​vi​e^i​
with e^i\hat e_ie^i​ the unit vector pointing in direction iii and viv_ivi​ the component of the vector in that direction. The basis vectors are such that e^i⋅e^j=δij\hat e_i\cdot \hat e_j = \delta_{ij}e^i​⋅e^j​=δij​. Two such vectors for which this is true are said to be orthonormal.

2.2 We live in R3\mathbb R^3R3 so a vector at a point (technically we say it is over the point) in this space is v⃗=ax^+by^+cz^\vec v = a \hat x+ b\hat y+c \hat zv=ax^+by^​+cz^ (in rectangular coordinates). If we assign such a vector to each point in R3\mathbb R^3R3 we get a vector field on R3\mathbb R^3R3,
F⃗(x,y,z)=a(x,y,z)x^+b(x,y,z)y^+c(x,y,z)z^. \vec F(x,y,z) = a(x,y,z)\hat x+b(x,y,z)\hat y+c(x,y,z)\hat z. F(x,y,z)=a(x,y,z)x^+b(x,y,z)y^​+c(x,y,z)z^.
That is, it is a vector valued function F:R3→R3F:\mathbb R^3\to \mathbb R^3F:R3→R3. This function is assumed to be smooth.

Here are some fancy words: A vector field is a section of the tangent bundle over some manifold. I’ve used the language of differential geometry - you might find MATH 481 to be useful if you want to get a better appreciation for the vector calculus you’ll be doing.

2.3 At the end of the day, we want to study how things change so we should think about how to differentiate things. Note first that a vector field may already be the derivative of a scalar function. A derivation that maps a scalar to a vector at each point is a gradient, ∇⃗=∂xx^+∂yy^+∂zz^\vec \nabla=\partial_x \hat x+\partial_y\hat y+\partial_z\hat z∇=∂x​x^+∂y​y^​+∂z​z^.

If F⃗(x,y,z)=∇⃗U(x,y,z)\vec F(x,y,z) =\vec \nabla U(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)=∇U(x,y,z) we say the vector field is a conserving field. Typically, the vector fields we consider will be gradients of scalar functions. You will see why in a week or two.

The gradient of a scalar function vanishes at what are called critical points. Such points are either local maxima, local minima or saddles. The classification of critical points is more nuanced in higher dimensions (d>2d>2d>2) than the second derivative test (see 2.4).

With $\vec \nabla $ being a vector, we can consider inner and cross products with vector fields:
curl(F)=∇⃗×F⃗(x,y,z)div(F)=∇⃗⋅F(x,y,z) \text{curl}(F) = \vec\nabla\times \vec F(x,y,z)\\ \text{div}(F) = \vec\nabla \cdot F(x,y,z) curl(F)=∇×F(x,y,z)div(F)=∇⋅F(x,y,z)

The curl tells you how quickly a vector field twists while the divergence tells you how something about the flow rate (flux) through some imaginary surface. This is all stuff you should know…

2.4 We can also consider various second derivatives. The most important is the Laplacian of a scalar function:
∇⋅(∇U(x,y,z)):=∇2U(x,y,z). \nabla\cdot(\nabla U(x,y,z)):=\nabla^2U(x,y,z). ∇⋅(∇U(x,y,z)):=∇2U(x,y,z).

You’ll probably see me write Δ\DeltaΔ for the Laplacian.

The geometric analog of the cross second derivative ∂2/∂x∂y\partial^2/\partial x\partial y∂2/∂x∂y is the Hessian. It’s defined as the Jacobian of a gradient. In components,
Hijf=∂2f∂xi∂xj. H_{ij}f=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^i\partial x^j}. Hij​f=∂xi∂xj∂2f​.
We won’t use the Hessian though it has an important role in dynamics, in particular it can classify critical points (there is an entire field of geometry built around this object called Morse theory). It is morally the ‘second derivative’ test - you look at the sign of the Hessian eigenvalues (which you’ll learn about).
